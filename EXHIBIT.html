01 — ABOUT

What is Trash to Art?

Trash to Art is an environmental support activity at Jose Rizal University (JRU) in Manila, Philippines. It initiates students creativity into building artworks, sculptures, and installations using discarded and recyclable materials.

More than just a campus project, it reflects JRU's commitment to ecological literacy, community awareness, and the power of art as a vehicle for social change.

02 — JOURNEY

The Creative Process

🗑️

Collection Drive ▶ Play

Students gather discarded materials — plastic bottles, tin cans, old newspapers, broken electronics, fabric scraps — from homes, campuses, and local communities.

🧪

Sorting & Processing ▶ Play

Materials are cleaned, categorized, and assessed for structural and aesthetic potential. Workshops guide students in safe handling and creative possibilities.

✏️

Concept & Design ▶ Play

Teams brainstorm and sketch concepts. Faculty mentors from Fine Arts, Architecture, and Engineering collaborate to guide technical feasibility and artistic vision.

🎨

Creation & Assembly ▶ Play

The transformation begins. Students build sculptures, murals, functional art, and installations using only reclaimed materials — no virgin resources allowed.

03 — MATERIALS

From Waste to Wonder

Plastic Bottles
→
Mosaic Sculptures

Plastic bottles are cut, flattened, and layered to create shimmering mosaic panels that mimic stained glass. When lit from behind, they produce a kaleidoscopic glow.

85% of artworks use plastic
📰
Newspapers & Cardboard
→
Papier-Mâché Figures

Old newspapers are pulped and molded into life-size figurines representing communities affected by waste pollution, creating powerful human-scale statements.

70% of artworks use paper
🥫
Tin Cans & Scrap Metal
→
Kinetic Wind Art

Tin cans are shaped into spinning turbine blades and mounted on armatures. Wind-powered kinetic installations move in real time, symbolizing energy renewal.

55% of artworks use metal
👕
Old Clothing & Textiles
→
Textile Installations

Discarded garments are woven into large-scale tapestries hung across campus corridors, each piece tagged with the story of its origin — a visual census of consumption.

40% of artworks use fabric
💾
E-Waste & Electronics
→
Circuit Board Portraits

Circuit boards, wires, and broken screens become intricate portrait collages — faces of environmental activists assembled entirely from the technology they warned us about.

30% of artworks use e-waste
🍶
Glass Bottles & Jars
→
Light Installations

Colored glass bottles are arranged into chandelier-like hanging structures. Solar-powered LEDs inserted inside create ambient lighting displays for campus events.

25% of artworks use glass
04 — IMPACT

Why It Matters

🌍

Environmental

Every kilogram of material used in Trash to Art is waste that doesn't end up in our oceans and rivers. The project directly reduces the campus's contribution to Metro Manila's 9,000-ton daily waste output.

📚

Educational

Students develop hands-on understanding of material science, waste cycles, and design thinking. The activity bridges classroom learning with real-world ecological responsibility.

🤝

Social

The exhibition is open to all strands of senior high school division at Jose Rizal University. This creates brand new social circles for interaction and collaboration.

🎭

Cultural

By treating waste as medium, the project challenges dominant narratives around consumption and value — art becoming the most powerful argument for sustainability.
